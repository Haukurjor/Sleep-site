How sleep cycles work

Sleep isn't one flat state. Through the night your brain repeats cycles of about 90 minutes, each climbing from light sleep down into slow-wave deep sleep and back up into REM, where most dreaming happens. A typical night holds four to six of these in a row.

The trick to waking up clear-headed is timing your alarm for the end of a cycle rather than the middle of one. Wake during deep sleep and you get that heavy, foggy feeling; wake as a cycle finishes and the same morning feels easy — sometimes even on slightly less total sleep. That's the timing this calculator works out for you, and why the recommended options are built around whole cycles rather than a flat "8 hours."

Common questions

How long is one sleep cycle?
A full sleep cycle lasts about 90 minutes, moving from light sleep into deep sleep and then REM. Most people go through four to six cycles a night, and the cycles aren't identical — deep sleep dominates early in the night, while REM stretches get longer toward morning.
Why do I wake up groggy even after a full night?
That heavy, foggy feeling is called sleep inertia, and it usually means your alarm went off in the middle of a cycle — often during deep sleep. Waking at the end of a cycle, when sleep is lightest, tends to feel much easier, even if it means slightly less total time in bed.
What is the best time to go to bed?
Work backwards from when you need to wake up: count back in 90-minute blocks and add roughly 15 minutes to fall asleep. Five to six cycles — about 7.5 to 9 hours — suits most adults. Enter your wake time above and the calculator lists the bedtimes that land on a cycle boundary.
How much sleep do adults need?
The National Sleep Foundation recommends 7 to 9 hours a night for adults aged 18 to 64, and 7 to 8 hours for adults 65 and older — roughly five to six full sleep cycles.
How much sleep do teenagers need?
Teens aged 14 to 17 need 8 to 10 hours — more than adults — because their bodies and brains are still developing and their internal clocks naturally shift later, making early starts harder.
How much sleep does my child need?
It changes quickly with age: newborns need 14–17 hours, infants 12–15, toddlers 11–14, preschoolers 10–13, and school-age children 9–11 hours. For children under six, a large share of that comes from daytime naps rather than night sleep alone.
Does this work for babies and toddlers?
For babies and young children the bedtime planner isn't shown, because their sleep is shorter-cycled and spread across naps. Instead you'll see the recommended daily total and a typical nap plan. The 90-minute cycle planner applies from about age six up. For a young child's actual schedule, your pediatrician is the best guide.
Is melatonin safe for children?
Melatonin is generally discouraged for young children and should only be considered for older kids under a doctor's guidance. It's sold as an unregulated supplement with inconsistent dosing, and accidental pediatric ingestions have risen sharply in recent years. Consistent routines and behavioral approaches come first.
